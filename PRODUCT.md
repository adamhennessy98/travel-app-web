# Product

## Register

product

## Users

YourWeekend serves **mixed primary intents**, reflected in the product’s three modes:

- **Plan a trip** — people who know where they’re going and want help shaping dates, pace, and the weekend around a destination.
- **Event-led travel** — people anchoring a trip on a festival, race, conference, or other dated event.
- **Inspiration-first** — people who aren’t sure where to go yet and want guided discovery before committing.

They use the product in **planning moments** (evenings, lunch breaks, “where should we go?” conversations), often on phone or laptop, with a need for **clarity** (dates, place names, next steps) and **emotional payoff** (excitement about the trip, not stress about the tool).

## Product Purpose

YourWeekend helps people **turn intent into a concrete trip** — from a city or event idea through search, saved plans, and shared views — without feeling like a generic booking engine. Success is: users trust the flow, understand what happens next, and leave with something they want to keep or share.

## Brand Personality

**Warm, playful, trip-forward.** Copy and UI should feel like a friend who loves travel: encouraging, specific, never corporate-cold. Delight is welcome when it supports the job (clear modes, memorable trip framing), not decoration that obscures actions.

## Anti-references

- **Generic SaaS / “AI product” monoculture** — purple-gradient hero clichés, identical icon+title+card grids, side-stripe accent borders used as fake structure, gradient text for headings, modal-first flows where inline or progressive patterns would serve better.
- **Interchangeable “template travel”** — surfaces that could belong to any category app with the logo swapped.

## Design Principles

1. **Three modes, one product** — Plan, event, and inspire stay visually and verbally coherent; no mode should feel like a bolt-on skin.
2. **Warmth through clarity** — friendly tone and hierarchy that reduce cognitive load; warmth is not noise.
3. **Travel context in every surface** — copy, imagery, and layout should read as *trips and places*, not abstract productivity.
4. **Trust on commitments** — money, dates, and sharing deserve conservative UX: obvious states, recoverable errors, no cleverness at the expense of comprehension.
5. **Accessibility as habit** — respect reduced motion, prefer readable defaults, and fix contrast issues as you find them; formal WCAG level can be tightened later.

## Accessibility & Inclusion

**Best-effort today** — no single WCAG conformance level locked. Still: prefer semantic structure, visible focus, keyboard-reachable controls, and copy that works with screen readers; avoid relying on color alone for meaning. When the product matures, revisit an explicit **AA** target for core flows.
